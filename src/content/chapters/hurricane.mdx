She woke at four-twelve.

Not the alarm. The alarm was set for four-thirty. These eighteen minutes were hers — not a gift, not rest, just the purgatory between sleep and the next thing she had to do. She lay still and listened to the house settle and to the refrigerator run through its cycle and to the cottonwood in the back yard that her mother had planted the year Audrey left for Vegas and that was now thirty feet tall and arguing with the morning wind.

She made coffee. She drank it at the kitchen table in the dark with the under-cabinet light on. She thought about what Linnea Aspen had said on the phone, which she had now played back so many times it had worn smooth — *if I'm not there you'll know because I won't be.* A woman who had thought through a thing in full before she said it. Audrey recognized the habit.

She left the house at five-twenty.

---

The drive down through Hurricane was the same road it had always been — State Route 9 west to the interchange, then the 9 again south through the wash country. An hour's drive to somewhere forty miles away, because the land in between required it. She took it at road speed. The Tahoe's air conditioning was working in her favor for once, which probably meant something was about to break.

She parked on the residential street she had mapped the night before, three blocks from the park. A cul-de-sac with a single streetlight out. She sat in the truck for a moment after she turned the key.

The street was still. A dog somewhere. The particular quiet of a town not quite awake.

She got out and walked.

---

The park was at the corner of 200 South and Main, which was the corner of not-much and a two-lane road that became something else a mile north. Two cottonwoods, a swing set, a wooden play structure faded to the color of old pine. A drinking fountain with a paper cup wedged under the spout by someone who had meant to come back for it. A green bench under the southwest cottonwood that had been in the exact same place long enough that the ground under its legs was pale where the sun never reached.

<Illustration src="/illustrations/ch05-bench-and-cottonwood.png" alt="Green bench under cottonwood, one figure, pen-and-ink editorial illustration." />


A woman of sixty-two in sneakers sat on the bench with an open book.

She did not look up as Audrey crossed the park.

Audrey sat down on the bench's far end — enough room between them for another person, if another person had been coming.

<Illustration src="/illustrations/ch05-two-on-bench.png" alt="Two women on a park bench, angled away, pen-and-ink editorial illustration." />


"Deputy," Linnea said.

"Linnea."

A car passed on Main. Then silence. Linnea turned a page. Audrey looked at the park's perimeter — the corner she had come from, the entrance to the residential street, the long sightline north along Main — and found nothing to look at. Then she looked again, because that was the thing she had learned in vice: empty lots are for people who are standing still. People moving don't look like people.

"He's chatty," Linnea said. She had not looked up from the book. "The man with the dog. He comes through at seven-twenty and stays fifteen minutes at least. He knows me by name."

"All right."

"And there is a girl who runs the Main Street loop. Seven-ten, seven-fifteen. She runs without headphones. If she notices two women on a bench together she will not say anything because she never says anything, but she will notice."

Audrey filed that. "Noted."

Linnea turned another page. She was not reading it. "Did you have any company on the way in."

"No."

"You checked."

"I checked."

A small pause. Not the teacher's pause — this was something else. A woman who had learned to trust her own instincts against the evidence of a clear road and was still not satisfied. She set the book face-down on her knee.

"I have had the feeling," she said, "for two days. It may be nothing. The last time I had it —" She stopped.

Audrey did not fill the silence.

"The last time I had it was five years ago, with Faith, and it was nothing. And Faith made it." She picked the book back up. "Tell me that means something."

"It means you pay attention."

"It means I am sixty-two and I am possibly inventing a threat because a girl I cared for is dead and I want there to be someone watching. It could be either."

"Yeah."

"But you should know."

"I know. Thank you."

Another car on Main. A truck, diesel, running north. Audrey watched it pass. It did not slow. She did not stop watching until it was past the intersection.

"I have done this twice before," Linnea said.

It was not the opening Audrey had expected — blunt, no throat-clearing, the way Linnea had answered the phone. She adjusted.

"Tell me."

"The first was eight years ago. A girl named Rebecca. Nineteen, same as Sariah. She is twenty-seven now. She works at LDS Hospital in Salt Lake. She sends me a card at Christmas with a picture of whatever she is doing that year. Last Christmas it was a ski trip. She was wearing sunglasses and grinning." A pause. "I do not know what that picture cost her. I know what it is worth to me."

"The second."

"Five years ago. A girl named Faith. She is in Phoenix. She does not send cards. She is alive. I have a friend in Phoenix who knows her and tells me twice a year, when I ask. He does not ask me why I want to know. I do not explain."

"Both made it."

"Both made it."

"What did you do differently with Sariah."

Linnea set the book on her knee again. She had not read a word since Audrey sat down. "Nothing. The plan was the same plan. The Cedar City shelter takes girls. The code words work. The timing on a Wednesday morning is the timing that gives the most room — shift change at the compound, mail delivery, the hour when there is the most movement and the most noise. I did nothing differently." She looked down at the book cover. "The household changed."

"What changed."

"I have a guess. I will not call it more than that. There is a woman in the Jessop house — the second wife. Marva. She is forty-one. She has a daughter who will be placed within the year. She runs the household's practical life. I heard about her from Sariah, in a call about three weeks before — " Linnea stopped. She smoothed the cover of the book, once, with her palm. "Three weeks before. Sariah said Marva had begun standing in the laundry while Sariah folded clothes. Not speaking. Just standing. It was a new thing."

"Marva found out."

"I think Marva found out enough. I do not know how." She looked up for the first time since Audrey had sat down. Her eyes were light-colored, gray-green, the kind that looked different depending on the light. Right now they were just tired. "I will not put that name on any official paper. You may write it in your notebook and take it with you and I will not stop you. I will not sign a statement and I will not testify to this in a courtroom. Not because I don't believe it. Because if I make that name official, I am no use to the next girl."

Audrey looked at her. "You're protecting your cover."

"I am protecting the work." Flat. Not defensive. "Write it in your notebook."

Audrey wrote it. *Marva Jessop, second wife, 41. Daughter, 14, pending placement.* She capped the pen.

"You mentioned a sister," Audrey said. "In the call last night."

Linnea was quiet for a moment. She looked back down at the book. "I said there may be a sister."

"Tell me."

"Sariah was careful for nine months. She did not slip. She did not tell anyone in that house. She knew what it would cost." Linnea smoothed the book cover again — the same motion, both hands this time, the way you smooth a letter you're about to fold. "But she was nineteen, and she was three weeks from leaving, and she loved the younger girls in that house the way older sisters love younger sisters — not wanting them to see it, not wanting them to carry it, but needing them to know. There is always one. The one she needed to know."

"A younger sister."

"Yes. I think she said something small. Not a plan, not a date, not anything useful. Something small and true. *I'm going to be all right.* Or something like it. And I think a younger sister heard her say it, and understood what it meant, and said nothing. And I think Marva was close enough to hear."

Audrey thought about that. She thought about the curtain in the upstairs window that Jared had mentioned — moving twice. She had clocked it when he said it and she had not written it down.

"Which sister."

"I don't know the names of all the younger girls. I know Sariah. I know Jared. The others I know by position, not by name. The sister who heard — she is young. Thirteen, maybe fourteen. She is sitting in that house this morning." Linnea set the book flat on the bench beside her, face down, no longer pretending. "She is not sleeping."

"No," Audrey said.

A silence opened between them that neither woman moved to close. The cottonwood overhead was shaking its top third in the wind that came off the hills at this hour, and the light through it was the new-morning light. A sparrow found the play structure's peak and assessed it and flew on.

She thought Linnea Aspen had been carrying a version of that weight alone for nine years.

She did not say this.

"The work you've been doing," Audrey said. "Rebecca, Faith, Sariah. No one knows."

"My son in Salt Lake does not know." Linnea picked the book up. "The woman in my book club — we read last Thursday, she chose the book, it's very good — she suspects something. She does not know what. She looks at me sometimes and she is adding something up that does not come out even. She is a good woman and she will not ask me until she does, and when she does I will decide what to say." "I have not had a friend in this town for nine years that I have not had to lie to. You are the first person I have told the truth to since before I started."

Audrey sat with that.

"Does that feel different," she said.

"Yes. Worse." Linnea said it simply, without complaint, the way you say the road is wet. "I expected relief. There isn't any. There is just — you knowing."

"That's usually how it works."

"Is it."

"Yeah."

Linnea looked at her for the first time in a long moment. The gray-green eyes, tired. Sizing something up or setting something down — Audrey couldn't tell which.

"How long have you been doing this," Linnea said. "The job."

"Twenty-three years."

"And before this case. When was the last time it felt like the right thing."

The question landed clean, without apology. A teacher's habit — the real question, not the polite one. Audrey thought of saying *a while* and did not.

"I don't know," she said. "I've been trying to find out."

"Mm."

The sparrow came back. It landed on the drinking fountain and looked at the paper cup and flew on.

"You said you're not near the line," Audrey said. "The line between going and stopping."

"I'm not."

"Even now."

"Especially now. Sariah is dead and the girl in her house who heard her say *I'm going to be all right* is still in that house. The line is not a comfort. It is just a fact."

"All right."

Linnea said, very quietly: "Were you near yours. Before this."

Audrey looked across the park toward the street she had come in on. The streetlight on the cul-de-sac that wasn't working. The houses with their curtains. She knew what the question was really asking, because she had read enough of what goes unsaid to read it here.

"Some days," she said.

"And now."

"Now I've got a case."

Linnea nodded. Not satisfied — but not pitying, either. "Yes," she said. "That is what it is."

Neither of them spoke for a while.

---

At seven-fourteen the girl who ran the Main Street loop came through the park. She was maybe twenty-five, ponytail, green shoes, and she did not look at the bench. Linnea watched her go. When she was past the cottonwoods Linnea said, without turning her head:

"There is one more thing."

"Tell me."

"I have been making the same walk every morning for seven years. The same park. The same bench. Same time." A pause. "This morning, on my way in, I passed the hardware store on Main. There is a truck I have not seen before. Parked facing north, which is the direction you came from. It was there at six fifty-five. It was there when I sat down." She turned the page of the book. Still not reading it. "It may be nothing. People park trucks in front of hardware stores."

"Make and model."

"White. Full-size. Not new. I could not see the plate from the sidewalk. I was not going to stop and look."

<Illustration src="/illustrations/ch05-white-truck-north.png" alt="White truck parked facing north, hardware store, pen-and-ink editorial illustration." />


"Which side of the truck was facing you."

"The passenger side."

Audrey thought. "Where on Main."

"Two blocks north of here. In front of the hardware store."

Which put the passenger window — and anyone watching from it — with a sightline directly south to the park's entrance on Main.

"All right," Audrey said. She stood. "I'm going to walk west along 200 South when I leave. Not back the way I came in."

"Yes. That would be better." Linnea opened the book to a new page — not the page she had been holding, a different one entirely, further back. As if she had finally found her place. "Go now. Don't wait for the man with the terrier."

Audrey went.

---

She did not look at the truck on Main as she crossed the intersection at 200 South. She looked instead at the hardware store's window and in its reflection she found what she was looking for: a white Ford, mid-nineties, dent in the passenger panel, and behind the windshield a shape that was not moving and was not doing anything that would attract attention except being there.

She walked west two more blocks without slowing.

Then she went around the block, came back on the residential side, and reached the Tahoe from the north.

She sat in the cab and wrote the truck description in her notebook below Marva Jessop's name and her daughter's age and the word *placement.*

She did not know if the truck was anything. She did not not know, either.

She started the Tahoe on the first try. She sat another thirty seconds, watching the street.

Then she drove.
